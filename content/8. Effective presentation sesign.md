**Effective Presentation Design** is built around the "iceberg concept," which suggests that while deep foundational knowledge is essential, the visible presentation must be carefully crafted to clearly communicate complex ideas.

The lecture outlines five core principles to achieve this:
- **Design for your audience:** Tailor your content to the audience's background, interests, and existing knowledge. You must also design your slides based on their purpose—whether they serve purely as visual aids, reading material, or a combination of both.
- **Break the curse of knowledge:** Experts often overestimate their audience's familiarity with a topic. To fix this, start simple, use everyday language and analogies instead of jargon, and highlight the practical relevance of your information.
- **Avoid cognitive overload:** Limit each slide to one core idea. Use slide titles to convey an assertion rather than a vague category, and include clear take-home messages. Organize data visually and use the "spotlight technique" to focus audience attention.
- **Communicate visually:** Graphics can reveal data better than statistical tables. Utilize clear plots, consistent color schemes, and pictograms to make the data more memorable and engaging.
- **Get the structure right:** Build a logical sequence with a clear introduction, body, and conclusion using established frameworks (like Issue/Action/Impact or Past/Present/Future). Crucially, "kill your darlings"—be willing to cut ideas or content you love if they do not serve the audience's needs.
