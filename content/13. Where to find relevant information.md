Now that you understand how scientific literature is structured and how to read it efficiently, the next step is actually finding the papers you need. The internet is flooded with information, but relying on a standard web search will yield unreliable, non-peer-reviewed results. 

To find credible primary and secondary literature, you must use specialized academic databases and search engines.

## 1. Major Academic Databases

Different databases serve different purposes. Some are broad and inclusive, while others are highly curated or discipline-specific.

* **Google Scholar:** The most accessible and comprehensive search engine for scholarly literature. 
    * *Pros:* It is free, intuitive to use, and searches across all disciplines. It is excellent at finding "grey literature" (like university repositories, technical reports, or conference proceedings).
    * *Cons:* It lacks strict quality control. It often indexes predatory journals or unverified manuscripts alongside rigorous peer-reviewed work, meaning the burden of evaluating credibility falls entirely on you.
* **Web of Science & Scopus:** These are the gold-standard, multidisciplinary databases for academic research.
    * *Pros:* They are highly curated. To be indexed here, a journal must meet  quality metrics, meaning you are generally guaranteed to be looking at legitimate, peer-reviewed science. They also offer incredibly powerful tools for tracking citations and analyzing research landscapes.
    * *Cons:* They are expensive, subscription-based services. You will need to access them through your university's library system.
* **PubMed:** The premier database for the biomedical and life sciences, maintained by the US National Institutes of Health.
    * *Pros:* It is free to the public and uses a highly structured tagging system (MeSH terms) that makes finding highly specific medical, biological, or clinical research incredibly efficient.

## 2. Accessing Paywalled Papers

Much of the world's highest-quality scientific literature is still published in traditional, subscription-based journals. If you click on a paper link from your personal internet connection, you will frequently hit a "paywall" asking you to pay $30 to $50 to download a single PDF. 

**Do not pay for individual articles.** There are several standard practices researchers use to bypass these barriers:

### Institutional Access
Your university pays millions of dollars annually for institutional subscriptions to major academic publishers. You can usually access paywalled papers legally through university libraries or institutional access.
* **Campus Wi-Fi:** If you are physically on campus and connected to the university's network, access is usually granted automatically via IP recognition.
* **Off-Campus (VPN/Proxy):** If you are working from home, you must log into your university library's portal, use institutional login. This routes your traffic through the university, authenticating your access and unlocking the PDF.

### Open Access and Repositories
An increasing number of papers are published as "Open Access" (freely available to everyone permanently). Additionally, as discussed in [[9. The landscape of scientific literature]], many researchers upload free **preprints** of their work to servers like arXiv or bioRxiv before formal publication. 

### Browser Extensions
Tools like **Unpaywall** or **LibKey Nomad** can be installed directly into your web browser. When you land on a paywalled journal page, these extensions automatically search thousands of university repositories and preprint servers in the background to find a legal, free, open-access version of the exact same paper.

### The Shadow Library: Sci-Hub
It is impossible to discuss accessing scientific literature without mentioning Sci-Hub. This "shadow library" bypasses publisher paywalls to provide free access to millions of research papers. 
* **The Reality:** While extremely popular among researchers globally (especially those without well-funded institutional libraries), Sci-Hub operates by violating copyright laws. Its use is legally contested and its domains are frequently blocked in many countries. 

> [!TIP] The Direct Approach: Contact the Author
> If you are not affiliated with a university and cannot find a free version of a paper online, find the "Corresponding Author's" email address (which is always listed publicly on the abstract page). Send a brief, polite email asking for a PDF copy of their paper for your research. Authors are legally allowed to share their published work privately, and the vast majority are thrilled that someone is interested in reading their research!
