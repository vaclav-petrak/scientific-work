To recognize a robust scientific theory, look for these five criteria (often referred to as **Kuhn’s Criteria** or general standard criteria):

### I. Empirical Testability
The theory must make predictions that can be tested against the natural world. If a theory involves supernatural elements or unobservable entities that _cannot_ interact with the physical world, it is not scientific.
- **Example:** **The Theory of General Relativity.** Einstein predicted that gravity would bend light. Astronomers measured the position of stars near the sun and found they had shifted as he predicted during solar eclipse
    
### II. Replicability 
Different researchers using the same methods should arrive at the same results.
- ***Example***: A study suggested that "power posing" could change your hormones (increasing testosterone, cortisole). When other labs tried to replicate the study with larger groups of people, they could not find the same hormonal changes.
    
### III. Predictive Power
A strong theory doesn't just explain the past; it predicts the future.
- **Example:** The Periodic Table. When Mendeleev organized the elements, he left gaps for missing elements. He predicted their atomic weights and properties years before they were actually found.

### IV. Parsimony (Occam's Razor)
Given two competing theories that make the same predictions, the simpler one (the one requiring fewer assumptions) is preferred. Extra assumptions increase the probability of error.
-  **Example:** **The Heliocentric Model.** To explain why planets sometimes appear to move backward in the sky, the ancient Earth-centered model required complex loops called "epicycles." The Sun-centered model explained the exact same observation simply: Earth creates the illusion of backward motion as it overtakes slower planets in orbit. 
- This is the rule of tie-breaking. It says: _"If two theories explain the data **equally well**, choose the simpler one."_
### V. Correctability
Scientific theories are never "final." They are always provisional. A scientific theory explicitly admits that it might be wrong. Pseudoscience often claims absolute, immutable truth.
- **Example:** Newton’s laws were the "gold standard.But it failed for objects moving near the speed of light. **Einstein’s Relativity** supersede Newton’s work in.

## 4. Science vs. Pseudoscience: Recognition Checklist

Use this comparison table to evaluate a claim:

|Feature|**Scientific Theory**|**Pseudoscience**|
|---|---|---|
|**Evidence**|Follows the evidence wherever it leads.|Starts with a conclusion, looks for confirmation.|
|**Criticism**|Embraces criticism as a way to improve.|Views criticism as a conspiracy or attack.|
|**Terminology**|Uses precise, defined jargon (e.g., "allele", "velocity").|Uses "technobabble" to sound scientific (e.g., "quantum energy vibrations").|
|**Change**|Evolves with new data.|Static; knowledge is often "ancient" or "rediscovered."|
|**Burden of Proof**|rests on the claimant.|shifts burden to the skeptic ("You can't prove me wrong!").|

## 5. Summary

To recognize a scientific theory, ask:

1. **Can it be tested?** (Is it falsifiable?)
    
2. **Does it explain known facts better than the alternative?**
    
3. **Is it consistent with other knowledge?**
    
4. **is it simple?** (Parsimonious)
