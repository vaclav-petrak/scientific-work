## Definition
Research is defined as a **systematic, rigorous investigation** designed to develop or contribute to **generalizable knowledge**.

It distinguishes itself from everyday problem-solving or simple data gathering by its adherence to the scientific method and its goal to expand the collective understanding of a field.

> **The Core Objective:** To bridge the gap between *what is known* and *what is unknown*.
## Key Characteristics of Scientific Research
For an investigation to be considered "research" in the scientific sense, it typically adheres to the following characteristics:

* **Systematic:** It follows a structured methodology and defined protocols. The process is not random; it follows a logical sequence (e.g., The Scientific Method).
* **Empirical:** It relies on hard, observable, and measurable evidence (data) rather than pure theory, belief, or dogma.
* **Critical:** It involves careful scrutiny of procedures and results. Researchers must constantly question their own assumptions and look for potential sources of error or bias.
* **Reproducible (Replicable):** The methods must be transparent and detailed enough that others can repeat the experiment to verify the findings.
* **Cyclical:** Research rarely ends with a final answer. It often raises new questions, leading to a continuous cycle of inquiry.

## The Research Spectrum: Types of Research
Research is often categorized by its primary goal: understanding fundamental principles or solving specific problems.

### 1. Basic Research (Fundamental/Pure)
* **Goal:** Driven by curiosity and a desire to expand knowledge boundaries.
* **Focus:** Understanding natural phenomena, laws, or theories.
* **Outcome:** General knowledge, theories, and principles.
* **Example:** *“How does the universe expand?”* or *“What is the genetic basis of cell division?”*
* **Commercial Viability:** Often not immediately apparent, but provides the foundation for future applications.

>[!NOTE] Focus: "Blue Sky" Research
> **Blue Sky** research is an extreme form of **Basic Research** where the real-world applications are not just distant, but completely undefined at the start. 
>
> It operates on a *high risk, high reward* basis: while many projects yield no practical use, those that succeed often revolutionize the world in unexpected ways.

### 2. Applied Research
* **Goal:** Designed to solve specific, practical problems affecting a group or society.
* **Focus:** Applying known theories to generate solutions.
* **Outcome:** Products, technologies, processes, or policies.
* **Example:** *“How can we improve solar cell efficiency to 30%?”* or *“Developing a vaccine for a specific virus.”*
* **Commercial Viability:** Usually high and immediate.

>[!NOTE] The Applied Origins of Quantum Theory
> While Planck's constant ($h$) is now the bedrock of abstract physics, its discovery was driven by a practical request from the **electric lighting industry**. Companies needed to know how to create the most efficient light bulbs. Planck studied "black body radiation" and found that existing theories failed. He introduced the radical idea that energy comes in "chunks" (quanta). Thus, a question about better light bulbs created **Quantum Mechanics**.
> 

## The Research Cycle
Research is an iterative process, usually following these stages:
1.  **Observation:** Identifying a phenomenon or a gap in knowledge.
2.  **Question:** Formulating a precise research problem.
3.  **Hypothesis:** Proposing a tentative explanation.
4.  **Experimentation/Data Collection:** Testing the hypothesis empirically.
5.  **Analysis:** Interpreting the data.
6.  **Conclusion:** Accepting or rejecting the hypothesis and sharing results.
