Now that you know which databases to use (as covered in [[13. Where to find relevant information]]), you need to know *how* to search them efficiently. Typing a simple phrase into a search bar often yields tens of thousands of results. To avoid drowning in irrelevant papers, researchers employ strategic search techniques to pinpoint the exact literature they need.

## 1. Advanced Filtering and Boolean Logic

Mastering a few basic search operators can drastically improve the precision of your literature discovery. Most academic databases support these fundamental tools to help you narrow down results:

* **Boolean Operators (AND, OR, NOT):**
    * **AND:** Narrows your search. Both terms must be present (e.g., `cancer AND immunotherapy`).
    * **OR:** Broadens your search. Useful for synonyms (e.g., `cancer OR oncology`).
    * **NOT:** Excludes specific terms to remove irrelevant results (e.g., `cancer NOT pediatric`).
* **Exact Phrase Searching:** Using quotation marks forces the database to find the exact sequence of words. Searching `"stem cell therapy"` prevents the system from finding papers that just happen to have the words "stem," "cell," and "therapy" scattered across different paragraphs.
* **Advanced Filtering:** Use database filters to narrow down your results by publication year or document type. Filtering by the last 1–5 years is essential for finding recent, cutting-edge developments, whereas expanding your search to older decades helps you identify the foundational research that established the field's core principles. As recommended in [[9. The landscape of scientific literature]], filtering your initial search specifically for **Review Articles** is an excellent way to enter a new field.

## 2. Citation Tracking (The "Snowball" Method)

Perhaps the most powerful practical instruction for discovering relevant literature is citation tracking. Once you find one highly relevant, high-quality article (often called a "seed" paper), you can use it to map out the entire research landscape on that topic by tracking its forward and backward citations.

### Backward Citation Tracking (Finding the Foundation)
* **How it works:** You explore the reference list (bibliography) at the end of your seed article.
* **Purpose:** This helps you identify the older, foundational papers, key methodologies, and historical context that your seed paper built upon. If multiple recent papers keep citing the same older article, that older article is likely a landmark study in the field.

### Forward Citation Tracking (Finding the Future)
* **How it works:** You use the "Cited by" links provided by databases like Google Scholar, Web of Science, or Scopus to see every newer paper that has cited your seed article since it was published.
* **Purpose:** This allows you to trace how a specific methodology or finding has been used, challenged, or expanded upon in recent years. It is the best way to find the most up-to-date research derived from your original seed paper.

> [!TIP] The "Citation Web"
> Think of literature search not as a linear list, but as a web. By combining forward and backward tracking of key articles, you can quickly move from a single review paper to a comprehensive understanding of an entire scientific debate.

## 3. Author Searching and Identifiers

Sometimes, the most effective way to find relevant literature is not by searching for a topic, but by tracking a specific expert in the field.

* **Tracking Key Researchers:** If you read a highly relevant paper, look up its authors. Searching for the **first author** (the researcher who conducted the bulk of the work, often a PhD student or postdoc) will typically reveal a highly focused trail of recent papers on that specific niche. Conversely, searching for the **last author** (the principal investigator or lab director) will provide a broader overview of the lab's overarching research themes and long-term trajectory. Both approaches yield valuable, highly specialized research without relying purely on keywords.
* **The Name Ambiguity Problem:** Searching by name can be frustrating due to common names (e.g., "Wang, J" or "Smith, A") or name changes over a researcher's career. To mitigate this, always use the specific "Author" search field in academic databases rather than the general keyword search bar.
* **The ORCID Solution:** To solve the name ambiguity problem definitively, the scientific community uses **ORCID** (Open Researcher and Contributor ID). This is a unique, 16-digit persistent digital identifier for individual researchers. If you know an author's ORCID, you can query it directly in databases like Web of Science, Scopus, or PubMed to pull up their exact publication record with absolute accuracy.

## 4. Optimizing Your Keywords

Scientific language is highly precise. The words you use to describe a topic might not be the jargon used by experts in the field.
* **Iterative Searching:** Your first search is rarely your best search. Pay attention to the specific terminology, acronyms, and keywords listed in the abstracts of the first few relevant papers you find.
* **Adapt and Re-search:** Swap your initial, broad search terms for these new, highly specific expert terms to dramatically improve the relevance of your next search query.
