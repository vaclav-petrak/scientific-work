## Course info
This course equips students with the competencies necessary for the successful completion of their research. It begins by introducing the philosophy of science and explaining the key principles of scientific work. 

The ability to communicate results is vital not only for disseminating information but also for refining knowledge, as it requires one to organize, shape, and clearly express complex ideas. Students will explore various forms of scientific communication, with a primary focus on oral presentations and peer-reviewed articles, though other formats such as patents will also be introduced. 

As clear and compelling data visualization is a requirement for successful poster presentations, we will demonstrate how effective graphing techniques help reveal information. The course concludes with lectures on ethical behavior, using examples of misconduct to underscore the importance of scientific integrity and the consequences of ethical violations.

## Topics

### Lecture 1: Foundations of Scientific Inquiry and Oral Communication

*This module explores what science fundamentally is, how research is conducted, and how to present your initial ideas effectively.*

#### Philosophy of Science 
- [[1. Introduction to the Philosophy of Science]]: Examines the definition of science and traces the historical shift from Aristotelian natural philosophy to Galilean empiricism.
- [[2. The demarcation problem]]: Explores the boundary between science and non-science. Discusses Karl Popper’s falsificationism with Thomas Kuhn’s paradigm shifts and cycles of normal science.
- [[3. What makes theory scientific?]]: Lists the five pillars of a robust scientific theory: empirical testability, replicability, predictive power, parsimony (Occam's Razor), and correctability.

#### Practice of Scientific Research
- [[4. What is research?]]: Defines research as systematic, empirical inquiry and distinguishes between basic  and applied research.
- [[5. Formulating a research question]]: Guidelines on identifying knowledge gaps and defining precise, testable questions.
- [[6. Induction and deduction in research]]: Compares the "top-down" theory-driven approach of deduction with the "bottom-up" data-driven, exploratory approach of induction.
- [[7. Career options in research]]: Outlines the traditional academic trajectory alongside industry R&D roles, government policy, and application specialist positions.

#### Oral Presentation as a Form of Scientific Communication
- [[8. Effective presentation design]] This presentation covers the core principles of effective presentation design and shows how to clearly communicate complex ideas to your audience.

### Lecture 2: Scientific Literature and Data Visualization

#### Peer-Reviewed Articles

* [[9. The landscape of scientific literature]]: Distinguishing between primary research (original experiments) and secondary research (review articles, meta-analyses).
* [[10. The peer-review process]]: How it works (the journey from submission to publication), its advantages (quality control), and its current challenges (bias, speed, reviewer fatigue).
* [[11. Anatomy of a scientific paper]]: Understanding the standard IMRAD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) for research articles versus the structure of a review article.
* [[12. How to read a scientific paper efficiently]]: Practical tips on why you shouldn't read a paper front-to-back like a novel (e.g., Title → Abstract → Figures → Conclusion).

#### Literature Search and Review

* [[13. Where to find relevant information]]: Introduction to major academic databases (Google Scholar, Web of Science, Scopus, PubMed). How to access paywalled papers legally through university libraries or institutional access.
* [[14. Smart search strategies]]: Practical instruction on discovering relevant literature by tracking forward and backward citations of key articles (exploring their reference lists and "cited by" links), often combined with advanced filtering to narrow down results.
* [[15. How to cite correctly]]: The difference between quoting and paraphrasing, avoiding accidental plagiarism.
